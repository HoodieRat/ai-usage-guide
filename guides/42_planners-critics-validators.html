Agentic Workflows

Planners, Critics, Validators

Combine distinct AI roles to plan work, stress-test drafts, and certify outputs before they ship.

Related guides: Start with Task Chains & Delegation and Agentic Mode Overview. For fact-heavy work, pair with Source Quality & Fact Checking and News & Recency. For numeric work, add Math & Calcs.
Policy check: Validators must log their approval for regulated outputs (finance, legal, health). If a validator cannot certify, the chain stops.

What you’ll learn

  • How to define planner, critic, and validator responsibilities without overlap.
  • Prompt patterns that provoke constructive disagreement and deeper review.
  • Governance artifacts that prove the chain met quality and compliance rules.

Prerequisites

  • Documented deliverable requirements and risk ratings.
  • Role matrix showing who owns each planner, critic, validator seat (AI vs. human).
  • Storage for transcripts, critiques, and validation logs.

When to use this triad

  • Use it when: quality stakes are high, facts or formulas matter, or multiple teams must sign off.
  • Skip it when: one-off brainstorming or low-risk tasks where a single review is enough.
  • Right-size it: start with Planner→Validator; add a Critic only if defects or rework remain high.

Planner → Critic → Validator walkthrough

Planner prompt

“Draft a plan for launching a customer migration campaign. Outline phases, owners, and success metrics.”

Critic prompt

“Given the planner’s proposal, identify top risks, missing stakeholders, unrealistic timelines, and compliance gaps. Suggest fixes.”

Validator prompt

“Review the plan and critic notes. Confirm each requirement is met, mark open issues, and assign red/yellow/green status with next steps.”

Step-by-step

  1. Scope the output. Document success criteria, constraints, risk tier, and non-negotiables.
  2. Author the role prompts. Version planner, critic, and validator instructions so their objectives do not overlap.
  3. Brief the planner. Provide the brief, reference docs, and constraints table; store the generated plan with a checksum or hash.
  4. Run the critic pass. Feed the plan and constraints to the critic; require risk ratings, fixes, and evidence requests for each finding.
  5. Validate and loop. Validator confirms fixes against acceptance criteria, sends targeted follow-ups if gaps remain, and records final status plus required approvals.
  6. Archive the packet. File the deliverable, critiques, approvals, log entries, and prompt pack in the designated repository.

Output contract

When you deploy this triad, ensure each run produces:

  • Role prompt pack: Versioned planner, critic, validator prompts plus instructions for reuse.
  • Decision trail: Table of critiques, resolutions, and who approved them.
  • Status summary: Validator verdict (green/yellow/red) with rationale and follow-up tasks.
  • Compliance appendix: Links to policies referenced, checklists completed, and human approvals.

Role prompt snippets

Planner: "You are designing the approach for {{project}}. Provide phases, owners, inputs needed, and success metrics. Highlight dependencies and assumptions."
Critic: "You audit the planner's proposal. List critical risks, missing data, unrealistic assumptions, and policy conflicts. Suggest concrete fixes for each."
Validator: "You certify readiness. Compare deliverables to acceptance criteria, confirm fixes were applied, assign status (green/yellow/red), and note required approvals."

Validator checklist

- All acceptance criteria met?
- Critic issues addressed or waived with approval?
- Compliance and policy requirements satisfied?
- Status assigned with rationale and next actions?
- Evidence stored with run ID?

Logging

Log run ID, prompts used, planner output hash, critic findings, validator decision, and human approvals. Keep the log searchable by project, status, and reviewer.

Metrics that matter

  • Critique closure rate: Percentage of critic findings resolved before final approval.
  • Revalidation count: Number of times validators send work back for rework.
  • Approval lead time: Time from planner output to final validation.
  • Policy exceptions: Count of validator waivers granted and their justifications.

Tips & pitfalls

  • Planner ignores constraints: Embed a constraints table in the prompt and have the validator reject plans that fail to cite it.
  • Critic feedback is shallow: Supply sample critiques, set a minimum issue count, and rotate critics to add fresh views.
  • Validator rubber-stamps: Force validators to reference evidence for each green status and schedule random human spot checks.
  • Prompt drift sneaks in: Re-run a prompt diff review weekly so planner, critic, and validator instructions stay aligned with policy.

Recap

Separating planning, criticism, and validation creates productive tension. Structure prompts, capture every decision, and enforce approvals to raise output quality.

Acceptance checklist

  • ✅ Planner/critic/validator prompts versioned and stored with the project.
  • ✅ Decision log completed with resolutions and evidence.
  • ✅ Validator status recorded alongside compliance artifacts.
  • ✅ Human approvals captured for high-risk deliverables.
